# Purple Peruvian

## Summary
Purple Peruvian is an heirloom fingerling variety native to the Andes mountains of Peru and Bolivia, characterized by its striking deep purple skin and violet flesh. This ancient cultivar was treasured by Inca royalty and remains prized today for its starchy texture, distinctive colour, and earthy flavour. The variety is ideally suited to roasting, frying, and chips.

## Agronomic notes
Purple Peruvian produces small, slender fingerling tubers with deep purple skin and violet flesh scattered with numerous eyes across the surface. The flesh can appear somewhat opaque or marbled. The variety is starchy and floury, making it well-suited to applications requiring firm, dry potatoes. The tubers develop vivid purple pigmentation from high levels of anthocyanins—natural compounds that turn slightly blue when cooked. The variety grows best in cool, well-drained conditions and cannot tolerate frost.

## Culinary notes
Purple Peruvian excels in roasted preparations, where its waxy, starchy texture and earthy, slightly nutty flavour shine. Commonly prepared by halving and roasting or slicing into chips, it also performs well in frying and boiling. The vibrant purple-to-blue colour and mild potato flavour make it popular in gourmet and specialty markets. Historically, Peruvian peoples boiled, mashed, roasted, and fermented Purple Peruvians, and ground them into almidón de papa (potato starch). It is excellent for potato salads and other cooked applications.

## Origin & history
Purple Peruvian is an ancient cultivar originating in the high plains and mountain slopes of the Andes in Peru and Bolivia, where wild potato relatives naturally grew in pre-Columbian times. The variety was particularly treasured by Inca royalty and held significant cultural importance in Andean societies. It was likely introduced to North America from Europe in the nineteenth or early twentieth century, where it is also known by European names such as Vitelotte Noire. Today it is widely cultivated in the United States and remains a staple in South American cuisine.
