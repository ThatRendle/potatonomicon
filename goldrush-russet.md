# Goldrush Russet

## Summary
Goldrush Russet is a mid-season, high-yielding potato developed by North Dakota State University and released in 1992. Bred from ND450-3Russ × Lemhi Russet parentage, it features brown russeted skin, white flesh, and excellent uniformity with few defects. Prized for its versatility in the kitchen and strong commercial appeal for both fresh market and processing applications.

## Agronomic notes
Goldrush is a medium-maturing cultivar requiring approximately 94 days from planting to harvest under optimal conditions. It demonstrates consistently high yield potential, averaging around 218 cwt/acre in commercial trials, with 75% of tubers falling into preferred size classes (A1–A3). The variety produces smooth, uniform tubers with very few external or internal defects, particularly excelling in hollow heart resistance. It has medium dormancy and good storability. Tubers should be harvested two to three weeks after vine kill to ensure proper skin set and improved handling characteristics.

Disease-wise, Goldrush shows good resistance to common scab and moderate resistance to verticillium wilt and silver scurf, making it reliable in most growing conditions. However, it is susceptible to late blight, early blight, soft rot, fusarium dry rot, and most potato viruses, requiring standard disease management protocols.

## Culinary notes
Goldrush is prized for its excellent flavour, very white flesh, and creamy texture, making it particularly suitable for home cooking and restaurant use. Its medium specific gravity (approximately 1.061) makes it an all-purpose potato. It excels at baking and boiling, where its texture remains stable. For French frying, it performs very well when processed fresh from harvest or after short-term storage. It is marketed fresh as both individual count cartons and in bulk for processing. Regional chefs favour it for its consistent quality and versatility across applications.

## Origin & history
Goldrush Russet was developed at North Dakota State University's Agricultural Experiment Station in Fargo, North Dakota. The variety was selected in 1981 and officially released in 1992. It was registered in Canada in 1999 (registration number 4905). The name reflects its golden-russet appearance and the high-yield characteristics valued in commercial production. Since its release, it has become established in production regions across North America, Australia, and Florida, where its consistent performance and versatility have made it a popular choice for both commercial growers and home gardeners.
