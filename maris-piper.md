# Maris Piper

## Summary
Maris Piper is the most widely grown potato variety in the United Kingdom, accounting for 16% of the planted area as of 2014. Bred at the Plant Breeding Institute in Cambridge and introduced in 1966, it was one of the first commercially successful varieties engineered to resist potato cyst nematode, a major pest. A true all-rounder, Maris Piper combines high dry matter and low reducing sugars—making it ideal for chips and fries—with excellent roasting and mashing qualities, and has been recommended by renowned chefs Heston Blumenthal and Tom Kerridge.

## Agronomic notes
Maris Piper is a maincrop variety that produces large, oval tubers with golden skin and cream flesh. It yields high and has become the dominant commercial potato in the UK since 1980. The variety's key agronomic advantage is its resistance to *Globodera rostochiensis* (potato cyst nematode) conferred by the *H1* gene, which provides complete resistance to UK strains. This resistance was achieved through a complex cross involving the wild potato CPC 1673—a source of nematode resistance selected from the Commonwealth Potato Collection—backcrossed with Ulster Knight, then crossed with the progeny of Arran Cairn and Herald.

However, Maris Piper has significant vulnerabilities: it is one of the most susceptible potato varieties to slug damage and is highly susceptible to common scab, a bacterial disease that causes corky lesions on the skin. Common scab management requires precise irrigation timing at the critical tuber-initiation stage.

## Culinary notes
Maris Piper has a fluffy, all-purpose texture that suits a wide range of dishes. Its high dry matter content and low reducing sugars make it an excellent choice for making chips and French fries; it is the preferred variety for triple-cooked chips among professional chefs. The variety also excels when roasted (Heston Blumenthal called Maris Piper "in a league of their own" for roast potatoes) and produces creamy, well-textured mashed potato. Beyond fresh use, the variety is suitable for processing into frozen or dehydrated products. Its mild, pleasing flavour contributed to its rapid adoption and enduring popularity with consumers.

## Origin & history
Maris Piper was developed by H. W. Howard and his team at the Plant Breeding Institute in Trumpington, Cambridgeshire, United Kingdom. The final cross was made in 1956, but the variety was not recommended by the National Institute of Agricultural Botany until 1966, following a decade of testing and multiplication.

The variety's breeding programme was part of a wider effort to find resistance to *Globodera rostochiensis*, potato cyst nematode, a major pest of UK potato production. Work began in 1941 when Conrad Ellenby screened over 60 wild potato species from the Commonwealth Potato Collection, identifying four resistant types belonging to *Solanum tuberosum andigena*. One of these, CPC 1673, proved suitable for crossing with cultivated varieties while retaining nematode resistance. Howard's team incorporated this resistance through a complex series of crosses, ultimately producing a high-yielding, nematode-resistant variety with excellent culinary qualities.

The name "Maris Piper" combines Maris (from Maris Lane in Trumpington, where the Institute was located) with Piper, chosen arbitrarily by Howard's son. The variety became commercially successful and has been the most widely grown potato in the UK since 1980, dominating the fresh market, processing sector, and chipping industry.
