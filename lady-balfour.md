# Lady Balfour

## Summary
Lady Balfour is a Scottish-bred second early potato introduced in 2001 by the Scottish Crop Research Institute (now the James Hutton Institute). Named after Lady Eve Balfour, a pioneering advocate for organic farming, this variety excels as an organic potato with outstanding disease resistance and very high yields. Its attractive cream flesh with pink tinges and oval form make it distinctive in the marketplace, and its exceptional dormancy allows for long-term storage without sprout suppressants.

## Agronomic notes
Lady Balfour is a second early variety that produces very high yields, making it particularly valuable for organic growers. It thrives exceptionally well in low-fertility conditions, allowing cultivation with reduced artificial fertiliser inputs. The variety demonstrates excellent resistance to the major potato diseases—late blight, common scab, and virus Y—making it one of the most disease-resistant varieties available and allowing it to be grown well without chemical treatments. Its tubers are medium-sized ovals with distinctive light buff skin splashed with pink. The dormancy period is extremely long, enabling storage for extended periods without reliance on sprout-suppressant chemicals, a significant advantage for organic producers.

## Culinary notes
Lady Balfour is a waxy potato with firm, creamy flesh that holds its shape exceptionally well when cooked. The flavour is mild and pleasant. Its waxy texture and firm flesh make it ideal for boiling, baking as jacket potatoes, mashing, steaming, sautéing, and roasting. The partly coloured tubers with their cream and pink flesh create an attractive appearance when halved or chunked for presentation. It is particularly prized by organic growers and consumers for its suitability to multiple cooking methods and its visual appeal on the plate.

## Origin & history
Lady Balfour was developed at the Scottish Crop Research Institute (SCRI, now part of the James Hutton Institute) in Scotland and introduced in 2001. The variety was named in honour of Lady Eve Balfour (1898–1990), a pioneering figure in the organic farming movement in the United Kingdom and founder of the Soil Association. The naming reflects SCRI's deliberate breeding strategy to create a potato variety suited to organic agriculture, combining multiple disease resistances with high yielding potential and the ability to thrive in low-input systems. The selection of Lady Balfour from SCRI's core breeding programme represents a significant achievement in organic potato breeding.
