# Pontiac

## Summary
Pontiac is an early maincrop all-purpose potato with light pink skin and white flesh, bred by the USDA in 1938 and widely cultivated across North America, Australia, and other regions. Prized for its versatility in the kitchen and its waxy texture that holds shape well in boiling and salads, it remains a reliable commercial and home-garden variety.

## Agronomic notes
Pontiac is a determinate variety producing tubers that mature at roughly the same time, typically ready for harvest 110–130 days after planting or when foliage yellows and stops growing. It yields moderate-to-good commercial quantities with medium-sized, round tubers. The deep-set eyes can become knobbly if soil moisture is uneven. It is susceptible to late blight and common scab, making disease management important in wet conditions.

## Culinary notes
Pontiac's waxy texture and moderate starch content make it excellent for dishes requiring shape retention—particularly salads, where it holds together well and absorbs dressings without becoming mushy. It boils well, roasts with good caramelization, and works beautifully in gratins, soups, and pan-fried dishes. It can be baked whole and is versatile for all-purpose kitchen use, though not ideal for fluffy mashed potatoes. The mild flavour and creamy texture when cooked appeal to broad culinary applications.

## Origin & history
Pontiac was developed by the United States Department of Agriculture at the Potato Experimental Farm in Lake City, Michigan. Selected in 1931 from a cross of Katahdin and Triumph varieties, it was released for commercial cultivation in 1938. The variety became widely distributed across North America and beyond. In 1945, a color mutant of Pontiac—the Red Pontiac—arose spontaneously in Florida through work by J.W. Weston; this red-skinned variant was later registered by the USDA in 1983 and became a distinct commercial variety, though the original pink-skinned Pontiac remains in cultivation, particularly in Australia.
