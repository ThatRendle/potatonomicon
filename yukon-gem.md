# Yukon Gem

## Summary
Yukon Gem is a mid-season, yellow-fleshed potato developed at North Dakota State University and released in 2006. It is a cross of Brodick (a disease-resistant Scottish variety) and Yukon Gold, inheriting improved yield and disease resistance from its parents. The variety produces medium-sized, oval tubers with pale yellow skin marked by characteristic red splashing and is valued for its excellent disease resistance and all-purpose culinary qualities.

## Agronomic notes
Yukon Gem matures in the early maincrop season and demonstrates consistently high yields that exceed its Yukon Gold parent by approximately 12 percent across multiple trial sites. Tubers are round-oblong with shallow eyes and good size distribution. The variety has short dormancy, allowing earlier sprouting than some competitors. Disease resistance is a defining characteristic: the variety shows strong resistance to Potato Virus Y (PVY), late blight, common scab, black spot, and tuber malformation—traits inherited largely from its Brodick parent. These resistances make it particularly valuable for organic production systems. The variety maintains moderately low specific gravity.

## Culinary notes
Yukon Gem tubers exhibit excellent cooking qualities with no after-cooking darkening. The medium-yellow flesh is appealing and consistent. The variety is versatile and all-purpose, suitable for boiling, baking, roasting, and processing into chips or fries. Culinary performance in post-harvest evaluations (boiling, baking, and microwave cooking) was comparable to Yukon Gold, the standard for yellow-fleshed varieties. The smooth skin and attractive light-yellow color make it well-suited to fresh market sales.

## Origin & history
Yukon Gem originated from a cross between Brodick and Yukon Gold made in 1994 at North Dakota State University in Fargo, North Dakota. The variety was initially selected from the F1 population in 1995 under the designation NDA5507-3Y. Development and evaluation proceeded through rigorous field trials conducted by the USDA ARS and the University of Idaho Potato Breeding Program, with collaboration from state universities in Idaho, Oregon, and Washington as part of the Northwest Potato Variety Development Program. After more than eleven years of evaluation across five western states, the variety was officially released and named in 2006. The name reflects its origin in the Yukon Gold line and its gem-like qualities. By 2007, Yukon Gem was being cultivated on significant acreage across western U.S. states and Canada for both seed and commercial production.
