# Russian Banana

## Summary
Russian Banana is a small fingerling potato with khaki skin and golden yellow flesh that originated in Europe's Baltic region in the 1700s. It became popular across North America after Russian sailors brought it via Pacific Northwest ports and is now one of the most widely cultivated fingerling varieties in the United States. Prized for its waxy texture, buttery-nutty flavour, and reliability as a heavy-yielding crop.

## Agronomic notes
Russian Banana is a late-season maincrop variety that is notably drought tolerant and a heavy producer, delivering very high yields. The small fingerling tubers are uniform in shape (oblong to crescent) and quick to mature despite the late classification. Plants show good general disease resistance and the variety is widely adapted. These potatoes are good keepers with medium dormancy, making them suitable for storage. The thin, waxy skin is rarely peeled, and the dense, moist flesh holds its shape well during cooking.

## Culinary notes
Russian Banana potatoes are prized for their waxy, moist texture and distinctive buttery, nutty flavour reminiscent of walnut or hazelnut. The thin skin does not require peeling and is often left on after scrubbing. They excel in applications that preserve their shape: boiling whole, roasting, steaming, baking in jackets, and cold potato salads. The golden flesh and firm texture make them visually appealing and ideal for dishes where tuber integrity matters. Their subtle nuttiness adds complexity to simple preparations.

## Origin & history
Russian Banana fingerling potatoes were developed in Europe's Baltic region during the 1700s. The variety was brought to North America by Russian sailors who transported seed potatoes through Pacific Northwest ports, including Alaska and British Columbia. Once established on the continent, Russian Bananas became an essential staple in North American cuisine and are now among the most commonly grown fingerling varieties in the United States. They remain widely available in markets across Europe and North America today.
