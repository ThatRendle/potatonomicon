# Yukon Gold

## Summary
Yukon Gold is a mid-season, high-yielding fresh market potato developed in Canada in the 1960s. Prized for its distinctive yellow flesh and thin, smooth skin, it offers excellent versatility in the kitchen with a naturally buttery, sweet flavour that requires minimal preparation. Widely cultivated across North America and beyond, it has become a staple home-garden and commercial variety.

## Agronomic notes
Bred to combine Peruvian potato characteristics (flavour and colour) with North American traits (size and smooth shape), Yukon Gold demonstrates high yield potential with a reduced dormancy period that shortens the time to market readiness. The variety produces medium to large, round to oblong oval tubers with characteristic buff-tan, slightly netted skin and medium-yellow flesh. Eyes are shallow to intermediate and not densely distributed. Specific gravity is low to medium (1.071), indicating a balance between starch and moisture — neither excessively floury nor waxy. The variety shows good resistance to mild mosaic and moderate resistance to leafroll virus, but is susceptible to common scab, PVY, early blight, dry rot, silver scurf, black scurf, and soft rot in storage. Standard disease management protocols are recommended.

## Culinary notes
Yukon Gold's all-purpose nature and naturally buttery, slightly sweet flavour make it exceptional for both everyday cooking and special occasions. The medium starch content and waxy-all-purpose hybrid texture allow it to hold its shape during boiling and mashing while developing a creamy, smooth consistency. It excels in potato salads, mashed potatoes, roasting, and baking. The thin skin requires little peeling, making preparation simple. Its signature golden colour and subtle sweetness have made it a favourite for both home cooks and restaurants seeking a premium fresh market potato.

## Origin & history
Yukon Gold was developed at the University of Guelph in Ontario, Canada, in 1966 by Garnet ("Gary") Johnston with the support of Geoff Rowberry and researchers from Agriculture Canada and the Ontario Ministry of Agriculture and Food. The cross between Norgleam (a North Dakota variety) and W5279-4 (itself a cross of Yema de Huevo and Katahdin) was deliberately designed to produce a potato with the flavour and colour of Peruvian potatoes but the larger size and smooth shape preferred in Southwestern Ontario. The variety was officially registered in Canada in 1980. The "Yukon" name references the Yukon River associated with the Klondike Gold Rush, while "Gold" describes the potato's distinctive golden-yellow flesh and buff skin colour.
