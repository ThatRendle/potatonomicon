# Spunta

## Summary

Spunta is an early maincrop ware potato with large, uniform tubers and excellent yield potential. Bred in the Netherlands in 1968, it has become one of the world's most widely cultivated varieties, particularly prized in warm and dry climates where its exceptional heat and drought tolerance allow reliable production.

## Agronomic notes

Spunta matures in the early maincrop season and produces very high yields of large, long-oval tubers with regular sizing. The variety demonstrates medium dormancy and produces 9–11 tubers per plant. It is resistant to potato cyst nematode (Globodera rostochiensis), a major pest in European production, and carries broad virus resistances, though it shows slight susceptibility to potato leaf roll and tuber-borne viruses. It is susceptible to late blight in foliage and shows resistance to wart disease. The variety performs exceptionally well in challenging conditions: heat and drought tolerance are key agronomic strengths that have made Spunta a preferred choice for cultivation in hot, dry regions worldwide.

## Culinary notes

Spunta is an all-purpose variety suitable for a range of culinary applications. With relatively high dry matter content (around 19.9%) and moderate starch (14%), it produces slightly mealy, versatile tubers ideal for mashing, baking, and frying. The light yellow flesh remains pale after cooking. In the traditional market, Spunta is reliable and consistent, though it is not recommended for industrial French fry or potato crisp processing. Its broad adaptation and forgiving cooking characteristics make it popular for everyday household use and restaurant cooking across diverse culinary traditions.

## Origin & history

Spunta was bred by J. Oldenburger in the Netherlands and introduced in 1968, arising from a cross of the Béa variety with USDA breeding line X 96-56. Initially, breeders saw little commercial value in the combination, but the resulting cultivar proved to be one of the most successful potatoes of the modern era. Its exceptional performance in warm, dry conditions led to rapid adoption throughout Southern Europe, the Mediterranean, North Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America. In Australia and other regions with challenging climates, Spunta became a standard choice for reliable, heat-resistant production. Today it ranks among the world's most widely grown potato varieties.
