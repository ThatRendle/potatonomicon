# Banana Fingerling

## Summary

Banana Fingerling is a late-maturing fingerling potato variety originating from the Baltic coast in the 18th century. Characterized by small, crescent-shaped tubers with khaki skin and golden flesh, it is prized by chefs and home cooks for its buttery, nutty flavour and waxy texture that holds shape beautifully in salads and roasted dishes. It is now one of the most widely grown fingerling varieties in North America, celebrated for its prolific yields and adaptability.

## Agronomic notes

Banana Fingerling is a late-maincrop variety requiring 110–125 days to maturity. It is extremely prolific and heavy-yielding, consistently producing abundant small tubers averaging 6–7 cm in length. The variety is well-adapted to diverse growing conditions and is notably drought tolerant, performing reliably even under moisture stress. Tubers are small and uniform, shaped like crescent-moons or gently curved fingers, with shallow to medium eyes and exceptionally thin, smooth skin that is rarely peeled before cooking.

The variety shows resistance to common scab but is susceptible to late blight, potato leafroll virus, and verticillium wilt. Good tuber set and a healthy production habit make it a favorite among commercial specialty growers, and it has been cultivated in North America (particularly British Columbia) for more than 90 years.

## Culinary notes

Banana Fingerling potatoes are renowned for their waxy, moist texture and distinctive full-bodied flavour profile that is pleasantly nutty with a buttery undertone—qualities that make them stand out from more neutral potato varieties. The thin, smooth skin is edible and rarely removed before cooking. When cooked, the flesh becomes fluffy yet creamy, developing a rich, buttery consistency.

Their versatility in the kitchen is exceptional. They are ideal for potato salads where they hold their shape beautifully, whether served warm or cold. They excel at roasting with herbs and olive oil, where they caramelize nicely, and are excellent for simple boiled preparations dressed with butter and fresh herbs. They can also be used in gratins, soups, and stews. The waxy texture makes them less suitable for mashing into fluffy mashed potatoes, though they produce a denser, creamier mash if desired. In traditional Baltic and Eastern European cuisine, they are paired with pork, served in cream-based bean soups, or accompanied by sour cream and cured fish.

## Origin & history

Banana Fingerling originates from the Baltic coast during the 18th century, when the region was part of the Russian Empire—hence the "Russian" designation. The variety gained its "Banana" name from its distinctive pale golden colour and gently curved, elongated shape, which faintly resembles a banana.

The variety spread across Europe via Baltic trading routes and subsequently reached North America through Russian fur traders who brought it to the Pacific Northwest and British Columbia in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It has been continuously cultivated in British Columbia for at least 90 years and became established in the broader North American market. Today, it is the most widely grown fingerling variety in the United States and is widely available as certified seed potatoes.

In Baltic and Eastern European cuisines, the Banana Fingerling has long been a staple, particularly in Estonia, Lithuania, and other regions where it is paired with pork, served in traditional soups and stews, and featured in regional dishes such as saltibarsciai (a cold beet soup).
