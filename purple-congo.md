# Purple Congo

## Summary
Purple Congo is a striking heritage potato with deep purple to blue skin and vivid purple flesh that retains its dramatic colour through cooking. A vigorous, late-maturing variety prized for its waxy texture and earthy-sweet flavour, it is particularly popular in Australia, the United Kingdom, and Canada among gardeners and specialty producers. Its prolific growth and disease resistance make it a favorite for organic cultivation.

## Agronomic notes
Purple Congo is a late-maturing variety that produces medium-sized, oblong tubers with lumpy, distinctive shapes. It is notably disease-resistant and vigorous in the garden, delivering prolific yields when given long periods of mild growing weather. The waxy flesh and low starch content mean the tubers hold their shape well during cooking. Sources indicate that while comprehensive agronomic data is limited, the variety is recognized for its durability and performance as a heritage cultivar maintained primarily by gardeners and seed enthusiasts rather than commercial breeding programs.

## Culinary notes
Purple Congo is classified as a waxy potato due to its low starch and high moisture content. The striking purple-blue flesh retains its colour when cooked, making it prized for visual appeal. It has a pronounced earthy, sweet, and nutty flavour with a dry, dense texture. Best used for boiling, steaming, roasting, salads, and gnocchi—applications where shape retention matters. It is not recommended for mashing (unless a chunky texture is desired) or deep frying. The tubers are often used in contemporary cuisine to create visually striking purple gnocchi and salad dishes.

## Origin & history
Purple Congo is a deep blue-fleshed potato of unknown origin, with little formal documentation of its breeding history. The exact breeder and year of introduction remain unconfirmed. The variety is closely related to other heritage blue and purple potatoes including British Columbia Blue, McIntosh Blue, River John Blue, and Sharon's Blue, suggesting it may originate from North America or the United Kingdom. Much of the variety's maintenance and development has been carried out informally by gardeners and seed conservationists rather than through institutional breeding programs, which has contributed to some confusion about nomenclature and exact provenance. It is now cultivated as a heritage variety in Australia, Canada, and the United Kingdom, valued by organic growers and culinary enthusiasts.
