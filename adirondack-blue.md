# Adirondack Blue

## Summary

Adirondack Blue is a mid-season potato released by Cornell University breeders in 2003. It is prized for its striking vibrant blue-purple skin and flesh that retain their colour when cooked. With a floury texture and mild, earthy flavour, it is valued by home gardeners and chefs seeking visually distinctive dishes with antioxidant-rich nutritional properties.

## Agronomic notes

Adirondack Blue is a mid-season variety maturing in 80–90 days with short dormancy. Tubers are oblong with intermediate eye depth and a fair to good appearance. The variety produces moderate yields with typical marketable yield distributions where 68% of tubers fall in classes A1–A3 (medium sizes).

The variety exhibits short dormancy and moderate storage potential. It is highly resistant to late blight, a major disease in potato cultivation, but has significant susceptibilities: it is highly susceptible to Fusarium dry rot and moderately susceptible to common scab, silver scurf, and golden nematode. It is also susceptible to several pests including Colorado potato beetle and leafhoppers, and to potato virus Y. Under Florida conditions, it showed slight susceptibility to hollow heart but no susceptibility to internal heat necrosis, corky ring spot, or brown rot.

## Culinary notes

Adirondack Blue tubers have low specific gravity (approximately 1.058) indicating lower starch content, resulting in a floury, moist, fine-grained texture. They are versatile in the kitchen: suitable for boiling, roasting, baking, mashing, and salads. A key characteristic is that their striking blue-purple flesh retains its colour even after cooking, unlike many other purple-fleshed varieties that fade. This makes them prized for visually appealing presentations in mashed potatoes, coloured salads, and chips. The flavour is mild and earthy with hints of sweetness and nuttiness.

The variety can be used for chipping but only from fresh storage—tubers from cold storage are unsuitable for chips.

## Origin & history

Adirondack Blue was released in 2003 by Cornell University's potato breeding programme, developed by Robert Plaisted, Ken Paddock, and Walter De Jong. The parentage is not recorded in available sources. It is part of the 'Adirondack' series of potatoes bred at Cornell, though unlike many of Cornell's modern varieties it does not possess broad disease resistance—notably, it lacks resistance to pink rot and Fusarium dry rot, two important potato diseases.

The variety's blue-purple coloration derives from anthocyanins, the same antioxidant pigments found in blueberries and purple cabbage, making it attractive to consumers seeking nutrient-dense foods.
