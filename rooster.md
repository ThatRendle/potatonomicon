# Rooster

## Summary
Rooster is a late maincrop potato with distinctive pinkish-red skin and yellow, floury flesh. Originally bred in Ireland in 1990, it has become one of the most widely cultivated potato varieties in the UK and Ireland, prized for its exceptional flavour, high yields, and versatility in the kitchen. The variety accounts for a significant portion of commercial potato production in its home country.

## Agronomic notes
Rooster is a vigorous late maincrop variety with consistently high yields, typically producing 40–50 tonnes per hectare under optimal growing conditions. Tubers are uniformly oval with medium size and distinctive pinkish-red/russet skin with shallow eyes, making them notably easy to peel. The variety exhibits long dormancy, storing exceptionally well, which contributed to its rapid adoption in commercial and home-garden production. By 2004, Rooster accounted for 38% of total potato production in Ireland, and by 2011 it comprised 56% of the area grown, reflecting its dominant position as the primary maincrop variety. Specific disease resistances are not widely documented in commercial sources.

## Culinary notes
Rooster is a general-purpose potato with high dry matter and floury texture, making it excellent for a wide range of culinary applications. The yellow flesh cooks to a light, fluffy consistency with a distinctive nutty, earthy, and buttery flavour. It performs exceptionally well when boiled, mashed, or roasted, and is highly favoured for chips and French fries. The variety is also excellent for jacket potatoes and baking. Its flavour profile and texture make it a favourite for both home cooks and professional chefs, contributing to its widespread popularity across the UK and Ireland.

## Origin & history
Rooster was bred in 1990 at the Teagasc Oak Park Research Centre in Carlow, Ireland, by Harry Kehoe. The variety resulted from a cross between the Irish selection OP 2532/64 and Pentland Ivory, a Scottish-bred cultivar. It was introduced commercially in 1991 and rapidly became the dominant potato variety in Irish agriculture, particularly during the 1990s when significant improvements in potato agronomy and storage infrastructure accelerated its adoption. The variety's combination of high yield, excellent storage characteristics, and superior flavour made it the benchmark maincrop potato for commercial producers and home gardeners alike.
