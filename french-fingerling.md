# French Fingerling

## Summary
French Fingerling is a small, waxy fingerling potato with a plump, long-oval shape and dusty-rose to red-pink skin. Developed in Brittany, France in the mid-20th century from a Rosa × Vale cross, it was commercialized by SICA Bretagne Plants in 1950. Prized for its low starch content, buttery texture, and ability to hold its shape during cooking, it is favored in gourmet and home cooking.

## Agronomic notes
French Fingerling potatoes mature as a late maincrop variety, requiring 90 to 125 days from planting to harvest. The plants are upright and leafy, reaching 60–91 centimeters in height. Tubers are small (averaging 7–10 centimeters in length) with a uniform, plump, long-oval shape and slightly tapered, curved ends, making them among the larger fingerling cultivars. The thin, smooth, delicate skin (dusty-rose to red-pink) is easily scratched or damaged, creating brown markings. Shallow eyes are typical. The golden yellow flesh is firm and dense; marbling in pink and red tones occasionally appears naturally during cultivation. The variety produces moderate yields and is selected for commercial cultivation for its distinctive size and noted resistance to common scab.

## Culinary notes
French Fingerlings are low-starch, waxy potatoes that cook creamy and moist while holding their shape firmly without disintegrating. The thin, edible skin does not require peeling. They have an earthy, subtly nutty, and buttery flavor suited to roasting, boiling, braising, and use in salads. They pair well with fresh herbs and spices, and are particularly valued in French cuisine for dishes such as ratatouille and bouillabaisse. Their small size and quick cooking time make them ideal for braising and whole-potato preparations. They are considered a gourmet variety in specialty produce markets and fine dining contexts.

## Origin & history
French Fingerling potatoes originated in Brittany, France, and were developed in the mid-20th century from a cross of Rosa and Vale potatoes. The variety was officially released in 1950 through SICA Bretagne Plants, an organization connecting potato growers, breeders, and distributors. The variety is also known as Roseval. It achieved early success in the French gourmet culinary market for its unique size and ease of preparation. By the mid to late 20th century, it had spread throughout Europe and was adopted in home gardens in the United Kingdom. The variety was introduced to the United States, where it gained a curious secondary name: Nosebag. According to local legend, agricultural educator Mark Fulford shared an account of a friend who traveled to France to purchase a racehorse. During a farm visit, the friend encountered French Fingerling potatoes and was impressed. Though French agricultural laws prevented the friend from legally carrying potatoes into the United States, a single tuber was discovered in the bottom of the racehorse's feedbag upon its arrival in America, allowing the variety to enter U.S. cultivation. The name later changed from Nosebag to French Fingerling for improved marketing appeal.
