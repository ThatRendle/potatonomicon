# Umatilla Russet

## Summary
Umatilla Russet is a late-maincrop russet-skinned potato bred in the United States and released in 1998, specifically engineered for frozen french fry processing. It produces high marketable yields with superior tuber uniformity and consistency compared to its close relative Russet Burbank, making it one of the major processing varieties grown commercially across North America.

## Agronomic notes
Umatilla Russet is classified as a medium-to-late maturing variety with a growing season suitable for most temperate climates. It consistently achieves high yields with excellent tuber uniformity and quality. Key agronomic strengths include resistance to net necrosis from potato leaf roll, tuber late blight, and common scab — three major potato diseases. The variety demonstrates moderate resistance to PVY (Potato Virus Y) and Verticillium wilt, offering improved disease management compared to some older russet types. Tuber-specific gravity is higher and more stable than Russet Burbank, contributing to reliable processing performance throughout storage. The variety produces long, oblong tubers with moderately shallow eyes and a uniform russetted, dark tan skin that appeals to both processors and fresh-market consumers.

## Culinary notes
Umatilla Russet excels in processing applications, particularly frozen french fries, where its high starch content, low sugar profile, and consistent specific gravity produce light, crispy fries with excellent colour hold-up during storage and preparation. The floury texture makes it excellent for baking and roasting, where the potato's dry matter breaks down into fluffy, creamy results. It is also acceptable for boiling, though the high starch content may cause some disintegration in long-cooking applications. The neutral, clean potato flavour makes it versatile across culinary applications where the potato's inherent character should not dominate the dish.

## Origin & history
Umatilla Russet was developed by Dr. J. J. Pavek at the University of Idaho Research Center in Aberdeen, Idaho, from a cross between Butte and an experimental selection designated A77268-4. The variety was formally released in 1998 by a tri-state partnership of the Agricultural Experiment Stations of Oregon, Washington, and Idaho, together with the United States Department of Agriculture Agricultural Research Service (USDA/ARS). It was bred in response to industry demand for a high-yielding, disease-resistant russet variety optimized for processing into frozen french fries. The potato was named by the state of Oregon after the Umatilla tribe, from which the city of Umatilla in Oregon also derives its name. Regional trials conducted between 1989 and 1991 demonstrated that Umatilla Russet achieved marketable yields averaging 40.5 Mg/ha, significantly higher than Russet Burbank's 32.4 Mg/ha across the same trials.
