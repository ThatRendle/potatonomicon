# Kipfler

## Summary
Kipfler is an Austrian potato variety prized for its distinctive elongated, finger-like shape and waxy texture. Named after the German word for "croissant" (*kipfel*), it is popular across Central Europe and has found favour in Australia as a premium salad and boiling potato. It holds its shape exceptionally well when cooked, making it ideal for dishes where presentation matters.

## Agronomic notes
Kipfler is a maincrop variety with moderate yields, producing medium-sized, long-oval tubers with light yellow to dusty yellow skin and corresponding light yellow flesh. The tubers have shallow eyes. The variety is not noted for particular disease resistance or abiotic tolerance in available sources, suggesting it requires standard potato husbandry. Its waxy texture is a distinctive agronomic trait that influences both growing habit and culinary performance.

## Culinary notes
Kipfler is best suited to boiling, roasting, and salad applications. Its waxy, firm flesh holds its shape during cooking—a defining characteristic that makes it excellent for potato salads, where it absorbs dressings without becoming mushy. It is not recommended for mashing or frying. The variety has a delicate, buttery flavour that works well in simple preparations that showcase its texture, such as boiled new potatoes or roasted wedges.

## Origin & history
Kipfler originated in Austria, though the exact date of introduction is not documented in available sources. The variety's name derives from the German *kipfel*, meaning "croissant," referring to its characteristic crescent or finger shape. It is known regionally as a German finger potato or Austrian Crescent. Variants exist across Central Europe, including the Kerkauer Kipfler from the Czech Republic and the Naglerner Kipfler from Lower Austria, which was registered as a cultivar in 1956. The variety has since spread to specialty markets in Australia and, to a lesser extent, North America.
