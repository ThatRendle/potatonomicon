# Jersey Royal

## Summary
Jersey Royal is a small, kidney-shaped new potato with pale yellow skin and creamy flesh, grown almost exclusively in Jersey and sold fresh in spring. It is highly prized for its sweet, nutty flavour and delicate waxy texture, and is protected by EU designation of origin. The variety is typically harvested young and sold fresh for just a few months each year, from early spring through late June or early July.

## Agronomic notes
Jersey Royal is a first-early variety, meaning it matures quickly enough to be harvested as a new potato. It produces small, uniform tubers with shallow eyes. The variety is moderately productive but is deliberately harvested young to preserve its delicate qualities. Jersey Royals show susceptibility to late blight, a common concern in cool, damp maritime climates such as Jersey's.

## Culinary notes
Jersey Royal potatoes are prized for their natural sweetness and subtle nutty flavour, with a dense, waxy texture that holds its shape during cooking. The thin, delicate skin is edible and often left on when boiling. Their low starch and high moisture content make them ideal for boiling and serving in salads, and they are frequently dressed simply with butter and herbs to allow their natural flavour to shine. Because they are picked young and fresh, they have superior flavour compared to stored varieties.

## Origin & history
Jersey Royals were discovered as a natural mutation in the late 19th century on the island of Jersey (Bailiwick of Jersey, English Channel). Farmer Hugh de la Haye purchased two large potatoes from a local store, each with fifteen or more sprouting eyes. He separated the eyes and planted each in a cotil (steep, sun-facing field) on his farm. When harvested, one eye produced distinctly different small, kidney-shaped tubers. De la Haye selected this mutation for cultivation in 1878, and the variety was introduced to British markets in the late 1880s, heavily marketed as a spring variety. The variety is known botanically as International Kidney. Jersey Royals have held Protected Designation of Origin status under EU law, recognising their unique association with the island's terroir. Jersey's mild climate, fertile soil, coastal air, and traditional use of vraic (seaweed) for soil enrichment have contributed to the potatoes' distinctive character.
