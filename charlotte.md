# Charlotte

## Summary
Charlotte is a second-early salad potato developed in France in 1981 and now widely grown across the United Kingdom and Europe. It is renowned for its excellent flavour, waxy texture, and ability to hold its shape during cooking, making it an ideal choice for potato salads and boiling. The variety received the Royal Horticultural Society's Award of Garden Merit in 2011.

## Agronomic notes
Charlotte is a second-early variety that reaches maturity in approximately 15–17 weeks under good conditions. Tubers are uniform, medium-sized, and oval in shape with smooth yellow skin and creamy-yellow flesh. The variety produces moderate yields of consistent, well-sized potatoes with shallow eyes and minimal waste.

Disease resistance is modest: the variety has some resistance to potato blackleg but is susceptible to late blight, dry rot, and common scab. It is not recommended for areas with high disease pressure without adequate fungicide management. The variety has low dry matter content (around 16%), classifying it squarely as a waxy salad potato rather than a floury maincrop type.

## Culinary notes
Charlotte is prized above all as a salad potato. Its waxy texture and firm flesh mean it holds its shape beautifully when boiled, steamed, or roasted, remaining intact even when cut and mixed with dressing. The flesh is creamy and finely textured, with a fresh, subtly sweet, nutty, and earthy flavour that is equally good hot or cold. The variety is also popular roasted whole for new potato dishes. It is less suited to mashing or baking than floury varieties, though small tubers can be baked whole.

In France, Charlotte is featured in the classic dish *tartiflette savoyarde*, baked with onions, bacon, cheese, cream, and wine. Across the United Kingdom, it has become one of the nation's top salad potatoes and is widely available in major retailers.

## Origin & history
Charlotte was developed in 1981 by breeder Roger Salaun at a laboratory within Château de Trévarez in Saint-Goazec, Finistère, Brittany, France. Salaun, an agricultural engineer who had worked at the laboratory since 1954, created the variety through a cross between the Hansa and Danaé cultivars. The breeding process was meticulous: Salaun planted over 40,000 tubers across ten years of selection before being satisfied with the new cultivar.

The official breeders are listed as UNICOPA and Société L. Clause (Sté Clause), French seed-potato and horticultural companies. Salaun deliberately chose the name "Charlotte" to appeal to the European market—the name is easily pronounced across many languages and carries associations with European royal lineages.

After its release in the late 20th century, Charlotte became a favoured variety for commercial growers and home gardeners throughout Europe. Its recognition culminated in the award of the Royal Horticultural Society's Award of Garden Merit in 2011, cementing its status as a benchmark salad potato.
