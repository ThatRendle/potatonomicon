# Kennebec

## Summary
Kennebec is a classic American all-purpose potato released by the USDA in 1948 and developed at the Presque Isle Research Station in Maine. Known for its high starch content and floury texture, it became a commercial success in chipping and table potato production across North America. Its creamy-buff skin and white flesh with shallow eyes make it instantly recognizable.

## Agronomic notes
Kennebec is an early maincrop variety maturing in 115–130 days, with yields of 400–500 cwt/acre under good conditions. It produces medium, oval to oblong tubers with smooth, creamy buff skin and shallow eyes. The plant is large, upright, and non-pigmented, with thick angled stems and broad, dark green leaves. Kennebec exhibits good to moderate resistance to late blight and several other diseases, notably potato wart and seed-piece decay. However, it is susceptible to common scab and highly susceptible to Verticillium wilt. The variety has a long dormancy period, making it suitable for storage.

## Culinary notes
Kennebec is prized for its floury texture and high starch content (approximately 22% starch by weight). When cooked, the flesh becomes soft, tender, fluffy, and slightly sweet—characteristics that make it versatile in the kitchen. It is an excellent all-purpose potato suitable for chips and fries, boiling, mashing, and baking. The high starch content allows it to crumble and absorb accompanying cooking liquids, making it ideal for both commercial chipping operations and home cooking.

## Origin & history
Kennebec was developed at the USDA Agricultural Research Station at Presque Isle, Maine, and was officially released to commercial growers in the United States in 1948. The variety was registered as a new cultivar in Canada in 1951. It is a cross between USDA B127 (itself a cross of Chippewa × Katahdin) and USDA 96-56 (a cross of Earlaine × USDA 3895-13). Following its release, Kennebec quickly became a successful chipping potato in both the United States and Canada, where tubers were sold domestically and exported as a processed chip ingredient.
