# Satina

## Summary
Satina is a versatile German all-purpose potato developed in 1981 as a cross between Puntilla and H99/73. It is a high-yielding, early-to-mid-season variety prized for its smooth, satin-like skin, excellent disease resistance, and exceptional drought tolerance. Originally developed in Hamburg, it is now grown commercially across Europe, North America, and Russia.

## Agronomic notes
Satina matures in 65 to 80 days, making it a reliable second-early potato for commercial cultivation. The variety is noted for its very high yields and produces small to medium tubers averaging 90–130 grams, with an oval to round shape. The skin is thin, taut, and smooth, with light netting, and the flesh is a rich dark yellow. 

The cultivar's most prized agronomic trait is its robustness: it shows outstanding resistance to common scab, tuber blight, and internal rust spot, and is resistant to potato cyst nematode (Ro1) and potato wart disease 1. Beyond disease resistance, Satina demonstrates exceptional drought tolerance and high resistance to second growth, making it adaptable to diverse soil types and climates. Its resilience to mechanical damage during harvest and handling makes it attractive for industrial potato production. The variety stores exceptionally well under cool, dry, dark conditions.

## Culinary notes
Satina is classified as cooking type B (waxy), producing a creamy texture when cooked while holding its shape well—ideal for boiling, mashing, and salads. The flesh has an earthy, nutty, and rich flavour profile. Chefs and home cooks appreciate its versatility across a wide range of recipes calling for standard potatoes, and it is particularly popular in European cuisines, especially in Germany and eastern Europe where it originated.

## Origin & history
Satina was bred in Hamburg, Germany, by Holger Laue working with Saka Pflanzenzucht GbR. The cultivar was developed in 1981 from a cross between the Puntilla variety and an experimental line designated H99/73. The name Satina refers to its characteristically smooth, satin-like skin and flesh. After its commercial release, the variety rapidly became popular in German and European home gardens and commercial agriculture. It was later introduced to North America, being formally registered in Canadian variety databases in 2010. Today, Satina is grown commercially and in home gardens throughout Europe, eastern Europe, North America, and Russia.
