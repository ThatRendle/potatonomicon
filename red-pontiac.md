# Red Pontiac

## Summary
Red Pontiac is a red-skinned, late-season potato variety with white, waxy flesh. Bred in Florida in 1945 as a color mutant of the original Pontiac, it has become the dominant red potato variety in North American production, accounting for approximately 65% of red potato acreage in the United States. It is prized for its consistent performance, ability to hold shape when cooked, and versatility in fresh market applications.

## Agronomic notes
Red Pontiac matures in 115–130 days, placing it in the late maincrop category. Tubers are uniformly round with smooth, dark red skin and white flesh; eye depth is shallow to medium, making peeling convenient. The variety produces moderate yields of 350–450 cwt/acre with a specific gravity of 1.065–1.075, indicating low to medium starch and a waxy cooking texture. It has moderate dormancy, allowing reasonable storage life under cool conditions. However, Red Pontiac is susceptible to late blight and common scab, requiring disease management and selection of clean seed stock.

## Culinary notes
Red Pontiac's waxy texture and low-to-medium starch content make it ideal for boiling, potato salads, and soups, where maintaining shape is desirable. It excels in applications where the tuber should not break apart during cooking. The variety is also suitable for roasting and baking; its mild flavour and creamy mouthfeel appeal to fresh market consumers. Because of its waxy nature, it is less ideal for applications requiring high starch content such as French fries or baking in the traditional sense, though it can be used for chipping. Many growers market Red Pontiac as a versatile fresh potato for home cooks and restaurant use.

## Origin & history
Red Pontiac arose as a spontaneous red-skinned color mutant of the original Pontiac variety, which itself was a hybrid of Katahdin and Bliss Triumph released in the United States in 1938. The mutation was discovered and selected by J.W. Weston in Florida in 1945. It was formally registered by the USDA in 1983. The variety was selected for its depth of red color and maintains the agronomic characteristics of its Pontiac parent. Since its release, Red Pontiac has become the standard red potato across North American markets, with its popularity extending to Canada, Australia, and several other countries. Its common synonym, Dakota Chief, reflects its historical significance in northern US potato production.
