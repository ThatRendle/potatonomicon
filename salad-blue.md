# Salad Blue

## Summary

Salad Blue is a heritage floury potato from Victorian Scotland, developed around 1900 through amateur breeding. Its distinctive deep blue skin and flesh retain their colour when cooked, making it a striking choice for chips, mash, and roasted wedges. Popular with chefs for its good texture and gentle flavour, it is an heirloom variety prized as much for its novelty appearance as its culinary qualities.

## Agronomic notes

A second early potato with medium-sized, oval tubers. The skin and flesh are uniformly deep blue or purple throughout, due to anthocyanin pigmentation—a compound with antioxidant properties. Salad Blue is described as vigorous and moderately productive, making it a reliable yield variety for home and specialty growers. The variety retains its blue colour well when cooked with the skin on. Like many heritage varieties, it is susceptible to late blight and other common potato diseases, so growers in high-disease-pressure areas should plan accordingly.

## Culinary notes

Despite its misleading name, Salad Blue is not a salad potato; it is a floury variety that crumbles easily in boiled water. It excels in applications where a light, fluffy texture is desirable: mashing, chipping, frying as crisps, roasting, and wedges. The potatoes should be cooked with the skin on to retain their striking blue colour, making them visually distinctive on the plate. Chefs appreciate the mild, delicate flavour and floury texture. The high dry matter content supports a satisfying, dense mash or chip.

## Origin & history

Salad Blue was developed in Scotland during the Victorian era, around 1900, through amateur potato breeding. The exact breeder is unknown. The name is historically confusing—it was likely marketed as a novelty or "salad" potato for its exotic blue appearance, though it is culinarily better suited to mashing and roasting. The deep blue colouring, rare among potato varieties, made it a curiosity in 19th-century British gardening and it remains a heritage heirloom grown by enthusiasts and specialty producers.
