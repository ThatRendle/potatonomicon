# Ranger Russet

## Summary
Ranger Russet is a medium-late maturing russet potato developed by the USDA-ARS and released in 1991 as an improved alternative to Russet Burbank for processing. It is prized for its high specific gravity, low reducing sugars, and superior disease resistance to several economically important pathogens. As one of the first major successes of the Tri-State variety development program, it has become widely grown across North America and beyond for frozen fry and fresh market use.

## Agronomic notes
Ranger Russet is an early-maincrop to medium-late variety with large upright to spreading plants, medium-thick green stems with red-purple pigmentation in full sun, and abundant red-violet flowers. The plant produces vigorous foliage and halts growth once flowering occurs. Tubers are long and slightly flattened with medium russeted brown skin, white flesh, and medium-depth eyes with distinctive eyebrows well distributed across the tuber surface. The variety exhibits high specific gravity (averaging 1.095 in Idaho) and medium tuber dormancy, making it well-suited for extended storage and processing pipelines.

Ranger Russet yields 10–20% higher than Russet Burbank and is notably resistant to Verticillium wilt, potato virus X and Y, leafroll net necrosis, and Fusarium dry rot. It shows good resistance to early blight, common scab, and leafroll virus, and is highly resistant to hollow heart and heat necrosis. However, the variety is very susceptible to late blight and root-knot nematode, and is moderately susceptible to growth cracks and blackspot bruises. It is resistant to metribuzin exposure, an advantage for weed control programs.

## Culinary notes
Ranger Russet is a floury potato ideal for frozen french fries, baking, and processing applications. The high specific gravity (1.095) and low reducing sugars at harvest and throughout storage result in lighter fry color and better processing quality than Russet Burbank, with minimal after-cooking discoloration. The white flesh cooks mealy and is suitable for baking but, like other floury russets, tends to break apart when boiled. It is acceptable for fresh market as well as processing, though its primary commercial use is in frozen fry production by major processors (Lamb Weston, Simplot, McCain).

## Origin & history
Ranger Russet was selected at the University of Idaho Aberdeen Research and Extension Center in 1977 from the cross Butte × A6595-3, designated as clone A7411-2 for testing. It was jointly released in 1991 by the USDA Agricultural Research Service and the Agricultural Experiment Stations of Idaho, Oregon, Washington, and Colorado. The variety represents the first major success of the Tri-State Potato Variety Development Program, which was established to breed improved russet replacements for the standard Russet Burbank. The designation "Ranger" reflects its role as a sturdy, robust variety suited to diverse growing conditions across the western United States. Ranger Russet has since become one of the most widely planted processing russets in North America and is also grown in Canada and Australia.
