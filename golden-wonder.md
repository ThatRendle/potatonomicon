# Golden Wonder

## Summary
Golden Wonder is a late maincrop Scottish heritage potato with rough golden-russet skin and pale lemon-cream floury flesh. Originally discovered in 1906 and beloved in British cuisine for generations, it is prized for its exceptional flavour and suitability for baking, roasting, and frying, though it requires care when boiling due to its floury nature.

## Agronomic notes
Golden Wonder plants develop a neat, upright habit growing to around 60 cm tall, producing attractive violet-blue flowers before setting their maincrop harvest. The variety has lower-than-average yields and produces medium-sized oval tubers with characteristic rough, golden-russet skin. While susceptible to some diseases, it shows reasonable resistance to blight and good slug resistance, making it moderately resilient in traditional British growing conditions. The variety is renowned for its good storage qualities, allowing it to be kept for several months under proper conditions.

## Culinary notes
Golden Wonder is a very dry, floury potato ideal for baking, roasting, and frying—it is particularly prized for making traditional British chips and homemade crisps. The high dry matter content contributes to excellent texture in these applications. However, it requires close attention when boiling, as its floury nature causes it to disintegrate in water if left too long. It is not recommended for boiling or salads. The variety is reputed to have the best flavour of all potato varieties and carries an old, earthy potato taste that makes it a staple in traditional British cuisine.

## Origin & history
Golden Wonder was originally discovered in the United Kingdom by Mr. Brown of Arbroath, Scotland, in 1906. It is a periclinal chimera, an unusual botanical phenomenon in which the outer layer of the tuber is the 'Golden Wonder' variety while the inner core is the 'Langworthy' variety. Despite common misconceptions, Golden Wonder is unrelated to the better-known King Edward potato. The variety has become deeply embedded in British culinary tradition and cultural identity, so much so that the Golden Wonder crisp company was named after it. It remains particularly popular in the United Kingdom, where it has a long history and secure place in traditional cooking.
