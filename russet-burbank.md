# Russet Burbank

## Summary
Russet Burbank is a late-maincrop potato with long cylindrical tubers, brown russeted skin, and white floury flesh. Developed by Luther Burbank in Massachusetts in the 1870s, it is now the most widely grown potato variety in North America, dominating commercial potato production in the United States and also cultivated in Australia. Prized for french fry production, baking, and dehydration due to its high starch content and excellent specific gravity.

## Agronomic notes
Russet Burbank matures in 130–150 days, producing large, long-cylindrical tubers (75–125 mm) with characteristic heavy russeted and netted brown skin. Eyes are shallow to medium, making peeling relatively straightforward. The variety exhibits high yield potential and excellent specific gravity (1.080–1.095), placing it squarely in processing-grade territory ideal for industrial french fry production. Dry matter content of 20–22% creates the floury, mealy texture when cooked.

The variety has long dormancy (120–150 days under proper storage at 7–10°C), enabling a single autumn harvest to supply processing pipelines through the following summer. However, Russet Burbank is susceptible to most major potato diseases including common scab, late blight, Verticillium wilt, and Potato Virus Y (PVY). It is also prone to physiological defects under stress—hollow heart, brown center, and growth cracks develop under excessive heat or moisture stress. Frost sensitivity requires careful timing of planting and harvesting.

## Culinary notes
Russet Burbank's high starch and dry matter content make it exceptional for french fries (producing crisp, golden-brown results) and baking, where tubers bake up dry and fluffy. The distinctive earthy potato flavor and slightly chewy skin are characteristic. However, the floury texture makes Russet Burbank unsuitable for boiling or salads, as tubers tend to fall apart. The variety is favored by fast-food chains and frozen-food processors; a large percentage of McDonald's french fries are made from this cultivar. Excellent for dehydration and mashing when texture breakdown is acceptable or desired.

## Origin & history
Luther Burbank (1849–1926), a self-taught horticulturist born in Lancaster, Massachusetts, bred the Russet Burbank. Between 1872 and 1876, working a small plot in Lunenburg, Massachusetts, Burbank experimented with seedlings from the Early Rose potato. From 23 seeds in a single Early Rose seed-ball, he selected one superior seedling for size, shape, and yield, naming it the "Burbank" potato. In 1875, Burbank sold his rights to the variety for $150 and relocated to Santa Rosa, California.

The original Burbank potato had smooth, light-tan skin. The russet-skinned mutation appeared spontaneously as a somatic sport in commercial Burbank fields in the early 20th century. By the 1920s, the russeted form had largely displaced the original smooth-skinned version, favored for its thicker, more scab-resistant skin and suitability for large-scale mechanical harvesting. Russet Burbank was not initially popular, accounting for only 4% of US potatoes in 1930; however, the introduction of irrigation in Idaho and the invention of frozen french fries in the 1940s and rise of fast-food restaurants in the 1950s drove explosive adoption. Today it accounts for approximately 25–40% of US potato planted acreage and 60–70% of Idaho's commercial production, making it the single most important potato variety in American agriculture. It is also the number one french fry spud worldwide and the most popular Australian variety for french fries and wedges.
