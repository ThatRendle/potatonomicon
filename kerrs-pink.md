# Kerr's Pink

## Summary
Kerr's Pink is a distinctive second-early floury potato with light brown skin and characteristic deep pink eyes, bred in Scotland in 1907. Prized for its excellent keeping quality and mealy texture, it is the second most popular potato variety in Ireland and is traditionally prepared with skin on for mashing, roasting, and boiling. The variety is gaining heritage status in some regions as modern cultivars displace it from commercial production.

## Agronomic notes
Kerr's Pink is a second-early maincrop variety with moderate yields, typically around 10 tons per acre. It produces medium-sized tubers with characteristically deep eyes that can make peeling challenging. The variety is valued for its exceptionally low water content and high dry matter, which makes it one of the best keepers of all potato varieties—tubers can store for several months. The plants grow quite tall, making the variety unsuitable for windy conditions. It produces many tubers but is susceptible to late blight and has only low resistance to common scab and potato cyst nematodes (both pallida and rostochiensis forms).

## Culinary notes
Kerr's Pink is a floury, starchy all-purpose potato excellent for mashing, boiling, roasting, and wedges. The cream-coloured flesh becomes soft and fluffy when cooked, though timing is critical—overcooking by even a few minutes will cause tubers to fall apart. Traditionally prepared with skin left on, it is a staple in Irish cuisine where it features in dishes like colcannon (mashed with kale or cabbage). The variety's low water content and mealy texture make it ideal for creamy dishes, soups, stews, and casseroles. Its mild, earthy flavour complements herbs and other ingredients without overpowering.

## Origin & history
Kerr's Pink was first introduced in Scotland in 1907 by J. Henry from Cornhill, bred from the cross Fortyfold × Smith's Early. The variety was originally called Henry's Seedling but took its current name from Mr Kerr, who grew it commercially in Ireland starting in 1917. It became hugely popular in Ireland, where it remains the second most produced potato variety after the Rooster. The deep eyes and irregular shape, which was initially a mark of the variety's character and a selling point, have contributed to its gradual displacement by more uniform modern cultivars in commercial production, though it retains strong following among home gardeners and in heritage potato movements.
