# LaRatte

## Summary
LaRatte is a French heritage fingerling potato known for its small, oval tubers with golden speckled skin and golden-yellow flesh. Prized by professional chefs for its unique nutty, buttery flavour and firm texture that holds shape beautifully during cooking, it is a specialty gourmet variety that commands premium prices at farmers' markets and fine dining establishments. It matures in 90–100 days and is resistant to common scab and viruses.

## Agronomic notes
LaRatte is a mid-season to second-early variety that reliably matures in 90–100 days from planting. The plants produce uniform, slender fingerling tubers of small to medium size with distinctive oval shapes and slight curves. Tubers are characterised by their golden speckled skin and golden-yellow flesh that maintains colour and firmness through cooking. The variety demonstrates good resistance to common scab and potato viruses. Yields are moderate and consistent; the variety was nearly lost from cultivation in the early 20th century due to low yields and disease susceptibility but was saved and revived.

## Culinary notes
LaRatte is a waxy potato that excels in applications where the tuber must hold its shape and texture: boiling, roasting, sautéing, braising, and potato salads are its primary uses. Its buttery, creamy texture when cooked and distinctive nutty flavour — often described as reminiscent of hazelnuts and chestnuts — make it a favourite of French chefs including the renowned Joël Robuchon, who famously used LaRatte to create exceptional potato purées. Its firm, moist texture also makes it ideal for rustic preparations and as a showpiece in haute cuisine plating.

## Origin & history
LaRatte originated in either Denmark or France in the late 19th century; most sources place its primary development in France, specifically in the Lyonnais and Ardèche regions. The variety was listed in the French official potato catalog in 1935 with an unknown original breeder. By the 1930s, the variety had nearly vanished from commercial cultivation due to seed degeneration and low productivity. It was dramatically saved from extinction in 1962 when a single surviving plant was regenerated at Le Touquet on the Côte d'Opale by French grower André Hennuyer. It was formally reintroduced to the market in 1965. The name "Ratte" is said to derive from the varieties of soils the potato grows in on French farms. Alternative names include La Reine, Asparges, Ratte du Touquet, and La Ratte de Paris.
