# King Edward

## Summary

King Edward is one of the oldest surviving potato cultivars in Europe, introduced in the United Kingdom in 1902. Prized for its excellent taste and distinctive light fluffy texture, it is widely considered one of the finest-quality potatoes for roasting, mashing, and baking. Though not the highest-yielding variety, its superior culinary qualities and reliable disease resistance have ensured its continuous cultivation for over a century.

## Agronomic notes

King Edward is a late maincrop potato requiring 20–22 weeks to maturity from planting. It produces medium-sized, oval tubers with creamy skin marked by characteristic red or pink blushes and spots. The variety has shallow eyes, making preparation easier than deeper-eyed varieties. It exhibits excellent resistance to common scab and moderate resistance to potato blight, though it is susceptible to potato cyst nematode. The variety has good keeping qualities and moderate yield potential; while not a high-volume producer, it is known for reliable, consistent performance in UK growing conditions. Traditional planting occurs in late March with harvest from mid-to-late August.

## Culinary notes

King Edward is renowned for its light, fluffy texture when cooked—a result of its floury starch profile. It is particularly prized by chefs and home cooks for roasting, mashing, and baking; the chef Delia Smith has described it as the best potato for making gnocchi. It is also suitable for chips/fries, sautéing, and steaming. The variety is often chosen by professional and amateur cooks alike for its superior flavour and versatility across nearly all potato dishes, except fresh salads where its floury texture is less desirable. Its excellent keeping qualities make it suitable for longer storage.

## Origin & history

King Edward was bred by a gardener in Northumberland who originally named it 'Fellside Hero'. The cultivar passed through the hands of a Yorkshire grower and a Manchester potato merchant before reaching John Butler of Scotter in Lincolnshire, who purchased all available seed stock and multiplied it across 50 acres. Butler renamed the variety King Edward in 1902, coinciding with the coronation of King Edward VII. Contemporary sources report that Butler sought permission from Buckingham Palace to name the potato after the monarch and received royal assent. The variety was developed from a cross of Magnum Bonum and Beauty of Hebron, though earlier historical accounts (including Redcliffe Salaman's 1949 work) listed the parentage as unknown. King Edward has remained continuously in cultivation and is one of the oldest surviving cultivars in Europe.
